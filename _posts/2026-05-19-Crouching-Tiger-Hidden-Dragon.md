# 《卧虎藏龙》

2026年5月16号周六, 赶上AMC电影院重影《卧虎藏龙》, 中文对白+英文字幕. 终于上了个中文电影, 必须捧场! 小影厅, 6排座位, 一共坐了5,6个人. 坐我前排的是个黑人老哥, 坐我后排一个老中和一个白人老头. 我心理合计这老外能看懂吗?

看惯了欧美快餐式的爆米花“大片”, 这次我走出电影院, 我才再次体会到什么叫“剧本的厚度”. 

> 顺路盘点一下近期上映的欧美电影: 
> 《Avatar: Fire and Ash》动画顶级, 剧情疑似流水账. 
> 《Project Hail Mary》拍的很不错.
> 《Michael》纯流水账, 但是个成功的流水账. 
> 《The Drama》一个R级别的电影, 可给我看恶心了, 看一半看不下去回家了. 
> 还有动画片我也没放过:
> 《Super Mario Galaxy Movie》小孩片.
> 《Hoppers》纯小孩片, 差点睡着.
> 《Zootopia 2》感觉导演想夹带私货, 又有点含沙射影.
>
> 不是说欧美电影不好, 动画、制作都是顶级, 细节把控也非常到位. 但精神内核普遍差了点意思. 

回到《卧虎藏龙》每一个场面都是经典, 每一句台词都值得回味. 从电影院出来回家, 我觉得这么有分量的剧本必须再看一遍. 周日下午在网上二刷. 周一晚上去AMC三刷. **《卧虎藏龙》绝对是我近十年来看过最棒的电影没有之一.** 



## 形式

总体说来, 取景、音乐、角色妆容、武术镜头全都无可挑剔. 电影第一幕我就知道来对了. 水墨画一样的开场, 取景自安徽黄山宏村. 让我打分我直接给到9/10. 除了口音有点出戏, 以我的观影体验, 还没发现任何硬伤. 

> 对了, 这里要点名批评AMC上周搬出的另一个Fan Fav电影: 2010年左右上映的《Crazy Rich Asians》. 角色妆容简直离谱, 眼线都快挑到后脑勺了都. 有个女的眼影涂的跟孔雀开屏似的🦚. 可能是我对东南亚华裔生活完全不了解, 但我认为与其说是描述华裔的生活, 不如说是西方刻板印象里的华裔. 一查导演出身, 果然ABC. 



## 角色与剧情

- 俞秀莲 (杨紫琼): 杨紫琼演这个角色我给到90分. 

  角色外貌、精神神态、略显粗燥的嗓音, 非常契合一个有生命力的江湖女武侠的形象. 唯一瑕疵是她口音太出戏了, 跟陈冠希一个味 <font color="grey">(*“微(wèi)信转账三(sàn)百块, 回香(xìang)港, 懂我意思吗(mà)？”*)</font>. 一开口就感觉空气里有东南亚的芒果榴莲味. 

  从俞秀莲角色来说, 我喜欢这个角色. 善良、聪明、隐忍、克制是她的底色. 面对青冥剑的失窃她处处留情, 即使到最后结尾挚爱因玉娇龙而死, 她也仅是把剑架在玉娇龙肩上, 选择了宽恕, 恩怨一笔勾销. 她的精神内核一直遵从儒家克己复礼的思想, 虽然现在看来过于保守, 但在当时就是主流价值观没问题的. 唯一可惜的是这种隐忍, 让她和李慕白直到最后一刻才彼此坦白.

- 李慕白 (周润发): 发哥演技肯定没问题, 但演这个角色有点串戏. 能看出发哥很努力在讲普通话, 但语音模型还是不太稳定. 打戏太少倒也合理, 武功高强没人能顶上两三招. 

  从李慕白角色来说, 武功盖世、爱才, 不必多言. 但武力值max却也让他性格更直, 目的性过强, 有些傲慢, 也过于莽撞. 丢宝剑那就登门玉府, 直接要回来. 看到玉娇龙是旷世奇才, 直言必须收她为徒. (人家都订婚要出嫁了, 说这些). 所以他看中的还是他自己的“江湖”秩序, 不能放任玉娇龙所谓的“毒龙”玷污武当派的名声. 罗小虎劫婚, 追上就逼问他碧眼狐狸下落, 对面是谁都没搞清楚, 多亏俞秀莲出手相助. 

  另外我最不理解的是在竹林和玉娇龙对打的时候, 李慕白居然漏出笑容. 这我真不理解. 你师妹左臂都被人刺伤, 还有啥心情笑? 玄牝剑法后继有人? 说不通. 网上有人说是李慕白爱上了玉娇龙? 这可太扯了. 反正整体上不太喜欢李慕白这个剧中角色.

虽然AMC选的logo是杨紫琼和周润发当封面, 但我觉得整部剧真正的主角只有一个, 就是玉娇龙. 她才是那条“藏龙”, 也是整部电影故事的精神核心. 李慕白和俞秀莲都是绿叶、铺垫、对比、映衬. 

- **玉娇龙** (章子怡): 这是我最近十年影视作品里最喜欢的角色. 她的精神内核就一个字“**真**”. 李安导演抓的非常准确. 

  现实里人人都想活成玉娇龙, 因她自由而潇洒, 但绝大多数人都是酒馆里的铁臂神拳、魁星五首之流. 要么平庸无能, 要么贪图虚名. 同时, 很多人不会成为玉娇龙, 因为代价太大了. 为了从罗小虎的山洞逃出去, 马都跑死了一匹, 水也喝光了. 万一没被人发现, 就会永远埋在沙漠里了; 如果偷剑之夜李慕白在场, 按李的性格多半当面直接拆穿盗剑者的身份, 不会像俞秀莲留住情面. 就算不是李慕白, 江湖“卧虎藏龙”, 蹦出个大侠直接抓住了怎么办. 直接身败名裂, 江湖梦都难以继续. 

  女孩子名字里带个龙字, 本身就承载了父母不寻常期望, 也注定了不平凡的一生. 父亲是封疆大吏, 后被提拔为九门提督 (相当于北京公安局局长). 玉娇龙出生在官宦大户里, 十辈子吃喝不愁的代价却让玉娇龙无法忍受: 与官家礼教的深度绑定. 婚姻在这种家庭是家族晋升的捷径, 自由恋爱更是做梦. 巨大的冲突激出巨大的火花. 玉娇龙八岁跟师娘碧眼狐狸偷偷习武, 一心追寻“江湖梦”.

  - 敢于亮剑, 挑战权威 (这是我最喜欢的原因)
    - 李慕白名声远扬, 还剑之夜她勇于亮剑. “相当我师傅, 谁知道你是不是浪得虚名?”
    - 罗小虎顶着半天云的名字劫道, 她母亲当场吓得晕倒. 但玉娇龙一点不惧, 上马直追罗小虎深入荒漠. 
    - 酒馆扯破虚名: 什么铁臂神拳, 魁星五首, 花影无踪飞刀长, 江南静玄禅师, 个个被打的鼻青脸肿, 一点面子不给.
  - 拒绝束缚, 追求自我
    - 李慕白与俞秀莲的爱是封建礼术下冰封雪藏的爱, 直到临死前才放下了守了一辈子的约束, 袒露心声. 与此对比, 玉娇龙和罗小虎的爱是炙热而的真挚的. 
    - 在镖局与俞秀莲对剑时, 双刀、双勾、红缨枪都被青冥剑斩断. 哪怕重鞭这种钝器都被切断. 俞秀莲说“有本事别用剑”, 出于江湖名声, 正常人会扔下武器. 但这何尝不也是江湖名誉的约束? 玉娇龙早就厌倦了江湖的虚伪.
  - 她需要的自由, 没有上限

## 终局

最后武当山那幕, 李安的设计比原著写的更有厚度, 是整部电影的点睛之笔.

罗小虎睡醒, 床上只有那把玉梳子, 但玉娇龙已经离开. 电影安排了一个绝美的超远景镜头. 桥上玉娇龙在模糊而昏暗的山峦背景下, 白色的衣服亮成一个格格不入的白点. 那抹白色, 是玉娇龙挣脱了束缚、活出的最存粹的自我, 是超脱灰暗的江湖人情、与世俗羁绊一尘不染的鲜艳颜色. 

“许个愿吧, 小虎.” 罗小虎的愿望是带她回新疆. 但他不懂, 新疆的茫茫大漠又何尝不是另一条束缚她的锁链? 玉娇龙不属于北京的深墙古宅, 也不属于广袤的新疆荒野. 

神话说心诚则灵, 她欣慰罗小虎依然爱着她. 可惜她无法带走这份爱. 玉娇龙没有遗憾, 平静的纵身跃入深谷、翱翔、最终融入大雾与苍茫, 藏于山峦沧海. 只有无牵无挂的终极自由才真正是她真正的归宿. 影片到这里结束, 李安导演的设计是顶级.





# Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon

Saturday, May 16, 2026. I caught a re-screening of *Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon* at an AMC theater, featuring Chinese dialogue with English subtitles. Finally, a Chinese movie is showing, so I had to go support it! It was a small theater room with 6 rows of seats, and only 5 or 6 people sitting in total. Sitting in front of me was a Black guy, and behind me were a Chinese guy and an older white gentleman. I wondered to myself, "Can these foreigners actually understand it?"

Used to Western fast-food-style popcorn "blockbusters," walking out of the cinema this time made me realize once again what "script depth" truly means. 

> A quick side-rundown of recently released Western movies: 
> *Avatar: Fire and Ash* — Top-tier animation, but the plot feels like a laundry list. 
> *Project Hail Mary* — Shot very well.
> *Michael* — A pure laundry list, but a successful one. 
> *The Drama* — An R-rated movie that actually disgusted me; I left halfway through and went home. 
> And I didn't skip the animated films either:
> *Super Mario Galaxy Movie* — A kids' movie.
> *Hoppers* — Pure kids' movie, almost fell asleep.
> *Zootopia 2* — Felt like the director was trying to smuggle in personal agendas and make innuendos.
>
> It's not that Western movies are bad; the animation and production are top-notch, and the attention to detail is exceptional. But the spiritual core generally falls a bit short. 

Back to *Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon*: every scene is a classic, and every line is worth savoring. Walking home from the theater, I felt that a script with such weight demanded another watch. I watched it a second time online on Sunday afternoon, and went back to AMC for a third time on Monday night. **Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon is absolutely the best movie I have seen in the last decade, bar none.**

Overall, the cinematography, music, character makeup, and martial arts choreography are all impeccable. I knew I made the right choice from the very first act. The ink-wash painting style opening, filmed at Hongcun in Huangshan, Anhui, was stunning. If I were to rate it, I'd easily give it a 9/10. Except for the accents breaking my immersion a bit, I haven't found any glaring flaws in my viewing experience. 

> By the way, I have to call out another Fan Fav movie AMC brought back last week: *Crazy Rich Asians* (released around 2010). The character makeup was simply outrageous—the eyeliner was drawn almost to the back of their heads. There was a woman whose eyeshadow looked like a peacock spreading its tail 🦚. Maybe I have absolutely zero understanding of Southeast Asian Chinese life, but I think rather than depicting real Asian life, it was more like depicting Asian life through the lens of Western stereotypes. I looked up the director's background, and sure enough, an ABC. 

## Characters and Plot

- Yu Xiulian (Michelle Yeoh): I give Michelle Yeoh a 90/100 for this role. 

  Her appearance, spirit, and slightly raspy voice perfectly match the image of a vital, martial arts heroine wandering the jianghu. The only flaw is her accent, which is very distracting—it has the same vibe as Edison Chen's <font color="grey">(*"WeChat transfer 300 bucks, returning to Hong Kong, know what I mean?"*)</font>. The moment she opens her mouth, it feels like the air smells of Southeast Asian mangoes and durians. 

  As a character, I really like Yu Xiulian. Kindness, intelligence, endurance, and restraint are her foundational traits. She shows mercy at every turn regarding the stolen Green Destiny sword. Even at the very end, when her true love dies because of Jen Yu, she merely places her sword on Jen's shoulder, choosing forgiveness and writing off all grievances. Her spiritual core always follows the Confucian ideology of self-restraint and restoring propriety. While it may seem overly conservative now, it was a perfectly fine mainstream value at the time. The only pity is that this intense restraint kept her and Li Mubai from confessing their true feelings to each other until the very last moment.

- Li Mubai (Chow Yun-fat): Chow Yun-fat's acting is undoubtedly fine, but he feels a bit out of place in this role. You can tell he is trying hard to speak Mandarin, but his phonetic model is still unstable. The lack of fight scenes makes sense, though—his martial arts are so supreme that no one can withstand more than two or three of his moves. 

  As a character, his peerless martial arts and his appreciation for talent need no further elaboration. However, having maxed-out combat power also makes his personality too direct, overly goal-oriented, somewhat arrogant, and reckless. Lost the precious sword? He just marches into the Yu residence and demands it back directly. He sees Jen Yu is a once-in-a-century prodigy, and bluntly declares he must take her as his disciple (even while she is already engaged and about to be married, he brings this up). Ultimately, what he cares most about is his own "jianghu" order; he cannot let Jen Yu's so-called "poisonous dragon" taint the reputation of the Wudang sect. When Lo hijacks the wedding, Li Mubai chases him down and interrogates him about Jade Fox's whereabouts without even figuring out who he is talking to. Thanks to Yu Xiulian stepping in to help. 

  Additionally, what I understand the least is Li Mubai actually smiling during his bamboo forest fight with Jen Yu. I truly don't get this. Your junior sister's left arm just got stabbed; what kind of mood are you in to smile? Pleased to find an heir for the Xuanpin Sword technique? It doesn't make sense. Some online say Li Mubai fell in love with Jen Yu? That is far too ridiculous. Anyway, overall, I don't really like Li Mubai's character in this film.

Although AMC chose Michelle Yeoh and Chow Yun-fat for their cover logo, I believe there is truly only one protagonist in this entire movie, and that is Jen Yu. She is that "Hidden Dragon," and also the spiritual core of the whole movie's story. Li Mubai and Yu Xiulian are simply green leaves serving as buildup, contrast, and foils. 

- **Jen Yu** (Zhang Ziyi): This is my favorite character in any film or television work from the last ten years. Her spiritual core can be summed up in one word: "**Authenticity**" (真). Director Ang Lee captured this perfectly. 

  In reality, everyone wants to live like Jen Yu because she is free and unrestrained, but the vast majority of people are just like the "Iron Arm" and "Flying Machete" guys in the tavern. Either mediocre and incompetent, or greedy for empty fame. At the same time, many people won't become Jen Yu because the price is simply too high. To escape Lo's cave, she rode a horse to death and drank all her water. If she hadn't been found, she would have been buried in the desert forever; if Li Mubai had been present the night she stole the sword, given his personality, he likely would have exposed the thief right to her face, rather than saving her dignity like Yu Xiulian did. Even if it wasn't Li Mubai, the jianghu is full of "crouching tigers and hidden dragons"; what if some great warrior just jumped out and caught her. She would be ruined, and her jianghu dreams would be difficult to continue. 

  A girl with the word "Dragon" (龙) in her name naturally carries her parents' unusual expectations, and is destined for an extraordinary life. Her father is a high-ranking regional official, later promoted to the Commander of the Nine Gates (equivalent to the Chief of Beijing Police). Jen Yu was born into a wealthy bureaucratic family, but having ten lifetimes' worth of food and clothing came at a cost she could not bear: a deep binding to official etiquette. Marriage in such a family is a shortcut for clan advancement, and fighting for freedom of love is a pipe dream. This massive conflict sparks massive fireworks. At eight, she secretly begins learning martial arts from her governess, Jade Fox, single-mindedly pursuing her "Jianghu Dream."

  - Dares to draw her sword and challenge authority (This is the reason I love her the most)
    - Li Mubai's reputation precedes him, yet on the night of returning the sword, she dares to draw her blade. "You want to be my master? Who knows if you're just living off an empty reputation?"
    - When Lo hijacks the convoy under the terrifying name "Dark Cloud," her mother faints on the spot. But Jen Yu isn't afraid at all; she mounts a horse and chases Lo straight into the desert. 
    - Tearing down empty vanity in the tavern: What Iron Arm, Flying Machete, Monk Jing—every one of them is beaten black and blue, giving them absolutely no face.
  - Refuses bondage, pursues the self
    - The love between Li Mubai and Yu Xiulian is a love frozen under feudal etiquette, only unsealed and confessed at the very brink of death after a lifetime of holding onto constraints. In stark contrast, Jen Yu and Lo's love is burning and sincere. 
    - During her sword fight with Yu Xiulian at the security bureau, twin blades, twin hooks, and spears are all sliced apart by the Green Destiny sword. Even a blunt weapon like a heavy whip gets cut down. Yu Xiulian says, "If you have real skill, don't use the sword." Out of jianghu reputation, a normal person would drop the weapon. But isn't that just another constraint of jianghu vanity? Jen Yu is already sick and tired of the jianghu's hypocrisy.
  - The freedom she needs has no upper limit

## The Ending

Finally, that scene at Wudang Mountain, Ang Lee's design adds so much more depth than the original novel, and is the finishing touch of the entire film.

Lo wakes up to find only the jade comb on the bed, but Jen Yu has already left. The movie sets up a breathtakingly beautiful ultra-wide shot. On the bridge, against the blurred, dim background of the mountains, Jen Yu's white clothes shine as a strikingly incongruous white dot. That splash of white represents Jen Yu breaking free from all restraints and living out her purest self; it is a vibrant color completely detached from the murky affairs of the jianghu and untainted by worldly attachments. 

"Make a wish, Lo." Lo's wish is to take her back to Xinjiang. But he doesn't understand, how is the vast desert of Xinjiang not just another chain meant to bind her? Jen Yu doesn't belong to the deep walls and ancient courtyards of Beijing, nor does she belong to the expansive wilderness of Xinjiang. 

The myth says that if your heart is sincere, your wish will come true. She is gratified knowing Lo still loves her. Unfortunately, she cannot take this love with her. Jen Yu has no regrets, calmly leaping into the deep valley, soaring, and eventually merging with the heavy fog and the vastness, hiding herself among the mountains and the sea. Only ultimate freedom with no attachments whatsoever is truly her real home. Ending the film right here, Director Ang Lee's design is top-tier.
